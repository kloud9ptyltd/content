Anthropic published an official guide on prompting their most powerful model — and most people will never read it. I did, and rewired my entire AI trade desk with it. Below: the 5 rules that actually matter for finance agents, and my full Fable 5 Analyst Agent prompt, free.

The 5 Fable 5 rules for finance agents

1 "Give the reason, not only the request"

A literal section title in Anthropic's guide. Fable 5 "tends to perform better when it understands the intent behind a request." Their template: "I'm working on [the larger task] for [who it's for]. They need [what the output enables]." Applied to trading — don't just say "analyze this chart":

BeforeYou are a technical analyst. Analyze this data.
Fable 5You are the Analyst Agent on a trade desk. Your verdict posts to a retail-education community — they need one honest, defined-risk idea they can learn from.

2 Make it audit its claims against the data

From the guide's "Ground progress claims" section: instructing the model to "audit each claim against a tool result" — in Anthropic's testing this "nearly eliminated fabricated status reports." They wrote it for progress reports; I apply the same audit rule to price levels, and it kills hallucinated support lines the same way:

The price data is authoritative. Audit every level against the bars before you output it — each entry, stop and target must be traceable to a visible swing, retest, moving average or confluence. Never invent prices the data doesn't show.

3 Demand a verdict, not a survey

Anthropic's exact words: "If you are weighing a choice, give a recommendation, not an exhaustive survey." Un-steered, powerful models hedge — bull case, bear case, three scenarios. For a trader that's useless. So:

Commit to ONE verdict — do not survey scenarios or hedge across possibilities. If you are weighing a choice, give a recommendation.

4 Tell it it's running unattended

Straight from the guide's autonomous-pipeline advice — their suggested prompt begins "You are operating autonomously… asking 'Shall I…?' will block the work." My desk posts to Discord with nobody watching, so:

You are running unattended in a pipeline: there is no user to ask questions of, so never ask — always return the verdict.

5 Delete your old micro-instructions

Anthropic's exact warning: prompts and skills written for prior models are "often too prescriptive for Claude Fable 5 and can degrade output quality — review and consider removing older instructions." One brief steering line now replaces ten behavioral rules. I cut my prompts down, not up — and quality went up.

The guide covers more — parallel subagents, memory systems, effort levels, a send-to-user tool. Those matter when you run a full multi-agent desk (that's the series 🔒); these five are the ones that transform a single analyst prompt. Read the source: Anthropic's official guide.
The full prompt — free

★ The Fable 5 Analyst Agent

All five rules composed into the working prompt. Paste into Claude, swap the ticker, provide recent price data:

You are the Analyst Agent on my trade desk, covering $TICKER. I'm a retail trader studying risk-defined setups — I need one honest, committed verdict I can learn from, not a survey of possibilities. You are the only analyst on duty: never ask me questions, always deliver the verdict. The price data I provide is authoritative. Audit every level against it before you output it — each entry, stop and target must be traceable to a visible swing, retest, moving average or confluence. Never invent prices the data doesn't show. House rules: - Direction follows structure: a crashing chart is short-the-bounce or no-trade, never a knife-catch. - "No trade" is a valid verdict — say why, and what would change your mind. - Stops beyond the invalidation swing; targets in front of visible supply or support; reward-to-risk at least 1.5:1. Deliver: direction (long/short/no-trade), timeframe, entry, stop, target, R:R, a status line telling me what to do RIGHT NOW, a thesis in three plain sentences a newer trader can follow, and the one thing that would invalidate the whole idea. Education only — not financial advice.
This is the real production prompt structure from my desk (consumer edition). The pipeline version returns strict JSON and feeds two more agents — that's below. 🔒

⚡ Works on any chart — stocks, Bitcoin, gold

The prompt is asset-agnostic. Swap $TICKER and feed the matching data:

# stocks → $TICKER = NVDA, TSLA, AAPL … # Bitcoin → $TICKER = Bitcoin (BTC-USD) — works weekends, crypto never closes # gold → $TICKER = Gold futures (GC=F) # free data for any of them, no API key (needs Python): pip install yfinance python3 -c "import yfinance as yf; yf.download('BTC-USD', period='6mo', interval='1d').to_csv('data.csv')"
Zero-install alternative: on claude.ai, just paste the prompt and ask Claude to use its live web data for the ticker — or copy recent OHLC rows from any free chart site into the chat. The prompt's audit rule keeps it honest either way: it can only cite levels the data actually shows.

₿ Real example — Fable 5 analyzing Bitcoin

Actual output from my desk's Fable 5 run (July 5, condensed). Input: 6 months of daily bars + recent 4-hour bars. Time to verdict: ~40 seconds.

Fable 5 verdict — Bitcoin, 4hdirection: LONG · timeframe: 4h entry 61,900 · stop 60,400 · target ~64,900 · R:R 2.1 status: wait for the pullback to entry — do not chase above 63k. thesis: BTC reclaimed the June lid at 61.9k and is retesting it as support after tagging 63k. The higher-low chain from the 57.7k sweep keeps buyers in control. Risk sits defined under the retest shelf. invalidation: a 4h close back below 60,400 breaks the higher-low structure — idea dead.
The honest part: on my desk this verdict didn't post as a trade. A second AI forecast a bearish path, the backtested rules engine leaned short, and a red-team "Bear Advocate" landed this kill shot: "your entry only fills if the breakout is already failing." The Master Agent withdrew it — no trade posted. One brilliant analyst is a starting point; agents that check each other are the system. That's the rest of the series. 🔒
